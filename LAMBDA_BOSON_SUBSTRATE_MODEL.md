## 2. The λ-Boson: Formal Definition

### 2.1 Mass from Oscillation

The λ-boson (Λ) represents the mass-equivalent of any electromagnetic oscillation:

```
Λ = hν/c²
```

Where:
- **Λ** (Lambda): Mass-equivalent [kg]
- **h**: Planck constant (6.62607015 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)
- **ν** (nu): Frequency [Hz]
- **c**: Speed of light (299,792,458 m/s)

Equivalently, in terms of wavelength:

```
Λ = h/(λc)
```

Where λ is wavelength [m].

### 2.2 Why "Boson"?

Photons are bosons — particles with integer spin that obey Bose-Einstein statistics. The λ-boson inherits this classification:

- **Spin**: 1 (like all photons)
- **Statistics**: Bose-Einstein (multiple λ-bosons can occupy same state)
- **Force carrier**: Electromagnetic force

The name "λ-boson" emphasizes that this is the **mass dimension** of the photon, derived from its wavelength (λ).

### 2.3 The Complete Photon Description

A photon is fully characterized by:

| Property | Symbol | Derivation |
|----------|--------|------------|
| Energy | E | E = hν |
| Momentum | p | p = h/λ = E/c |
| Mass-equivalent | Λ | Λ = hν/c² = E/c² |
| Wavelength | λ | λ = c/ν |
| Frequency | ν | ν = E/h |

The λ-boson framework focuses on the **mass dimension** (Λ) as the computational primitive.
